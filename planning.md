## 1. Situation Summary

- **Current status:** Ed.M. student on Fulbright, J-1 visa; must depart the U.S. at program end.
- **Prior experience:** ~5 years in Pakistan's education sector (Teach For Pakistan fellowship + related work).
- **Goal:** Land a role in international education development, ideally based in Africa or fully remote for a non-U.S. employer, before or shortly after graduation.
- **Compensation target:** Net USD equivalent of PKR 400,000–500,000/month (~USD 1,400–1,800/month net take-home, or ~USD 20,000–27,000/year net). This is a low bar for most international development roles based in Africa or fully remote INGO roles; the real question is *quality of role*, not just meeting the number.
- **Family:** Spouse must be accompanied; her work authorization / visa options must be part of every country decision.
- **Constraints:** No U.S. base of employment. J-1 two-year home residency requirement will be satisfied non-consecutively per USEFP confirmation.

---

## 2. Guiding Principles

1. **Two-track hunt from Day 1.** Track A: Africa-based in-country roles. Track B: fully remote roles with global/African INGOs, research firms, or education companies. Do not let one track crowd out the other.
2. **Build proof, not just promises.** Every semester left should yield at least one tangible output (paper, report, blog post, dataset) that a hiring manager can click on.
3. **Optimize for the first two years, not the perfect job.** A well-placed 18–24 month role at a credible org in Nairobi, Kigali, Kampala, Dakar, Addis, Accra, or Abuja will unlock the *next* role far faster than holding out for the ideal title.
4. **Treat the spouse question as a filtering criterion**, not an afterthought. Countries differ hugely on spouse work rights.
5. **Runway matters.** Have 3–6 months of savings by graduation so you can accept the right role instead of the first role.

### Phase 2 — Network Activation & Country Research (Aug–Oct 2026)

**Objective:** Convert your Vanderbilt/Fulbright standing into 30+ real conversations with people already doing the work, and narrow Africa to a shortlist of 2–3 countries.

**Actions**

1. **Map your Vanderbilt Peabody alumni network.** Use Vanderbilt's alumni database and LinkedIn to identify every IEPM alum (past ~10 years) working in Africa or at the target organizations. Prioritize alums 3–8 years out — they hire people like you and remember what the transition felt like.
2. **Map the Fulbright network.** Fulbright alumni in Pakistan and internationally are a warm channel. Use the Fulbright alumni directory and USEFP's alumni network. Reach out to Pakistani Fulbright alumni now working in international development.
3. **Run 20–30 informational interviews over 8 weeks.** Cadence: 3–4 per week. Script: 25 minutes, three questions — (a) how did you enter this role; (b) what's changing in your organization/country; (c) who else should I talk to. Always ask for two more names.
4. **Narrow Africa to a country shortlist of 2–3.** Score countries against a rubric:
   - Density of education-development employers
   - Spouse work-rights & spouse-visa policy
   - Cost of living vs typical local-hire salary
   - Ease of work-permit sponsorship for non-nationals
   - Safety & quality of life
   - English or French working language
   Realistic first-tier candidates: **Kenya (Nairobi)**, **Rwanda (Kigali)**, **Uganda (Kampala)**, **Ghana (Accra)**, **South Africa (Johannesburg/Cape Town)**. Second-tier: **Ethiopia (Addis)**, **Zambia (Lusaka)**, **Tanzania (Dar/Dodoma)**, **Nigeria (Abuja)**.
5. **Draft a country diligence brief (1–2 pages each)** for your 2–3 shortlisted countries covering: employer density, permit rules for skilled foreign hires, spouse dependent-visa provisions and whether the dependent can work, banking/tax basics, housing costs, healthcare, and the practical Pakistani community footprint.
6. **Talk to your Fulbright officer at USEFP** to get written confirmation (in email, filed) of the non-consecutive residency interpretation, and to understand whether time spent in a third country counts toward that requirement — this affects your career flexibility for years.

**Expected outputs**
- 20–30 informational interview notes filed and searchable
- A ranked country shortlist of 2–3 with a written rationale
- One 1–2 page country brief per shortlisted country
- Written confirmation from USEFP on residency-requirement mechanics
- Warm intros to at least 5 people currently at target organizations

**Key considerations**
- **Spouse visa realities:** Kenya's dependent pass historically allows the spouse to apply separately for a work permit; Rwanda is generally accommodating; South Africa's spousal visa allows work with an endorsement; Nigeria and Ethiopia are more restrictive. Verify directly — rules change and consular practice varies.
- **Local-hire vs expat-hire compensation:** Most African country offices of INGOs pay "national" or "local hire" scales to non-national early-career staff, which are much lower than "international" packages. Your salary floor (~USD 20k–27k net) is achievable at local-hire level in most target cities, but international-hire packages (housing, schooling, hardship) are rare for a fresh Master's graduate.

### Phase 3 — Skills, Portfolio & Practicum (Sep 2026–Jan 2027)

**Objective:** Leave Vanderbilt with a résumé that shows the skills the market actually pays for — and one or two visible pieces of work an employer can read.

**Actions**

1. **Choose electives strategically.** Prioritize: quantitative methods (regression, impact evaluation, causal inference), MEL / program evaluation, education economics or education finance, and one qualitative or systems course. Avoid loading up on abstract theory that doesn't translate to a job listing.
2. **Get one deep tools skill to the "I can be paid for this" level.** Realistic options: **Stata** (still the lingua franca of development research), **R** (increasingly common, free), **Python for data analysis**, or **Power BI/Tableau for MEL dashboards**. Pick one and go deep.
3. **Complete one credible certification.** Candidates: DIME/World Bank Impact Evaluation training, MEAL DPro (PMD Pro's MEL cousin), IPA / J-PAL online courses on RCTs and measurement, or a UNICEF/UNESCO IIEP short course on education policy.
4. **Use the IEPM practicum deliberately.** The practicum is a hiring pipeline if you pick the right host. Push for a placement with a target-list organization in East or Southern Africa (remote or in-person over a summer or J-term) rather than a U.S. host. If a U.S. host is unavoidable, pick one whose portfolio is African education (e.g., R4D, EDC, RTI education practice, Room to Read HQ).
5. **Publish 2–3 short LinkedIn essays or Medium posts.** Topics drawn from your coursework and research assistantship: e.g., "What Pakistan's enrollment data taught me about the limits of policy design," "Three things Kerala and Bangladesh get right that Punjab does not," "Why 'stakeholder buy-in' is not the same as co-design." Your first-semester LinkedIn post already sounds like this — keep the cadence.
6. **Convert your work with Dr. Brent Evans / Dr. Vishesh into a portfolio artifact.** Even if publication is far off, a 3–5 page working paper or memo, hosted publicly (SSRN preprint, personal site, or ResearchGate), is a hiring signal.
7. **If time allows, begin French at A1/A2.** Duolingo + Alliance Française. Even elementary French roughly doubles your Africa footprint and is genuinely useful in Rwanda, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire.

**Expected outputs**
- Transcript-visible quant/MEL coursework
- One tool (Stata/R/Python) at a demonstrable level, with a public GitHub or portfolio project
- One completed certification with certificate
- A practicum placement at a target-list org (ideal) or a U.S. org with African education programs
- 2–3 published LinkedIn essays
- One public working paper or policy memo
- (Stretch) French at A1/A2

**Key considerations**
- The market rewards *specificity of skill* over *breadth of theory*. Two mediocre skills lose to one strong one.
- Do not use your practicum on something you already know how to do. Use it to buy access to a target employer or a target region.

### Phase 4 — Active Applications (Nov 2026–Mar 2027)

**Objective:** Run a disciplined, high-volume, high-quality application campaign.

**Actions**

1. **Set weekly application quotas.** Realistic pace: 5–10 high-quality applications per week, plus 2–3 informational-interview asks. Track everything in a single spreadsheet: role, org, country, source, date applied, contact leveraged, status, next step, deadline.
2. **Tier applications into three buckets:**
   - **Reach:** International-hire roles at UNICEF, UNESCO, GPE, World Bank (unlikely at Ed.M. level, but the UN's Junior Professional Officer / JPO program and UNICEF's NETI program are worth checking against Pakistan's sponsoring quotas).
   - **Target:** Program Officer / Research Associate / MEL Specialist / Consultant roles at INGOs, research firms, and consultancies in East/Southern/West Africa. This is your center of gravity.
   - **Fallback:** Remote roles with education-tech, ed-consulting, or research firms that don't require a specific work location. Also, Pakistan-based roles at INGO country offices (UNICEF Pakistan, Save the Children Pakistan, Malala Fund, ITA, Aga Khan Foundation Pakistan) that would still satisfy the Fulbright home-residency clock.
3. **Fellowship-style entry programs** — apply to all that fit:
   - UN JPO (via Pakistan's mission if a slot exists)
   - UNICEF NETI
   - World Bank Young Professionals Program (very selective)
   - Rangel/Payne/Pickering-style U.S. programs are U.S.-only, so skip.
   - Mastercard Foundation Scholars alumni programs (if eligible)
   - Aga Khan Development Network fellowships
   - Overseas Development Institute (ODI) Fellowship Scheme — highly relevant, places economists in African government finance/planning ministries.
4. **Cultivate 3–5 consulting relationships as backup.** Short-term consulting gigs (2–8 weeks) with research firms or small INGOs can bridge May 2027 to your first permanent role, and they build the CV. Devex "consultants" board, LinkedIn ProFinder, and direct outreach are the channels.
5. **Every application gets a real cover letter.** No generic templates. One paragraph that names the specific country/program, one that maps your Pakistan experience to their theory of change, one that closes with a specific ask (interview, coffee, referral).
6. **Track and follow up.** 10 days after applying, follow up via LinkedIn to a real person on the team.

**Expected outputs**
- Applications tracker with 80–150 applications by mid-March 2027
- 3–5 active consulting-relationship threads
- 10–20 interviews (rough conversion of 10–15%)
- One fellowship application submitted per open program you qualify for

**Key considerations**
- **U.S. funding turbulence:** USAID contracting has been highly volatile in 2025–2026. Do not put more than 25% of your application weight on USAID-funded roles until the funding picture is confirmed at the time of applying.
- **Application timing:** UN, GPE, and World Bank rolling processes can take 6–9 months from application to start. Apply *early* — a March 2027 application likely means an offer in late 2027 or 2028.
- **Local-hire packages** will not always cover relocation. Budget from your own savings for the initial move.

### Phase 5 — Interviews, Offers & Logistics (Feb–May 2027)

**Objective:** Convert interviews into an offer aligned with your goals, and prepare a legally and logistically clean exit from the U.S.

**Actions**

1. **Run structured interview prep.** For each interview, prepare: (a) 5 STAR-format stories from Teach For Pakistan / research assistantship / practicum, (b) 3 questions specific to the role that show you've read their strategy document / annual report / recent RFP.
2. **Do compensation homework** before any offer conversation. Benchmarks:
   - Devex salary reports
   - Glassdoor and LinkedIn Salary
   - Direct asks to informational-interview contacts ("what's the realistic band for a Program Officer at [org] in Nairobi?")
   Frame your ask in local currency net, not PKR. Employers do not care about your Pakistan reference point.
3. **Diligence the offer before signing.** Confirm in writing:
   - Job title, salary, currency, tax withholding responsibility
   - Housing / relocation / repatriation policy
   - Work-permit sponsorship: who files, who pays, expected timeline (in Kenya this can be 3–6 months; Rwanda 4–8 weeks)
   - Spouse dependent-visa support; spouse work-rights under that visa
   - Health insurance including spouse
   - Home-leave allowance
4. **J-1 exit logistics.**
   - Confirm the last date of your DS-2019 program end and your 30-day J-1 grace period.
   - Book departure travel *within* the grace period.
   - Verify with USEFP once more, in writing, that your interpretation of non-consecutive residency is correct and how your third-country employment period will be documented.
   - Retain copies of all DS-2019s, I-94 records, transcripts, and program-completion letters.
5. **Spouse planning.** File the spouse's dependent-visa application in parallel with your work permit. Do not travel to post before her visa is in hand unless the employer's HR expressly says the family can follow.
6. **Financial preparation.**
   - Save an emergency reserve of 3–6 months of your target monthly net (~USD 5,000–10,000).
   - Open an account that works internationally (Wise, Revolut) for pre-arrival transfers.
   - Understand Pakistan tax residency rules — if you spend >182 days in Pakistan in a fiscal year, you may be a tax resident there even if working abroad.

**Expected outputs**
- One or more written offers
- Clean, dated exit paperwork from Vanderbilt/DSO
- Approved work permit and spouse dependent visa
- 3–6 month personal financial cushion

**Key considerations**
- Do not sign an offer without a written commitment on spouse-visa support. This is where deals fall apart.
- If your top offer requires a start date before your J-1 grace period ends, you will need to depart the U.S. and enter the host country on a business/visitor visa first, then convert. Confirm this pathway with the employer's HR *before* signing.

### Phase 6 — Transition & Launch (May–Aug 2027 onward)

**Objective:** Land clean, integrate fast, and set up the next 24 months of career compounding.

**Actions**

1. **Physical transition.** Depart the U.S. within your J-1 grace period. If you have a small gap before your role starts, route through Pakistan or a third country rather than overstaying.
2. **First 90 days on the role.** Standard playbook: listen more than you speak, meet every stakeholder, deliver one small visible win by day 60, publish one internal memo or dashboard by day 90.
3. **Fulfill the Fulbright home-residency service in a way that matches your USEFP agreement.** Keep documentation (letters from employer, tax records, entry/exit stamps) proving your qualifying time.
4. **Keep hunting quietly.** Set a review every 6 months: are you still on the trajectory? Are you building toward the next role (Manager → Senior → Advisor)?
5. **Publish and speak.** One conference presentation or public writing piece per year. This compounds.

**Expected outputs**
- Successful transition out of U.S. within J-1 grace period
- A documented, defensible completion of Fulbright residency obligations
- A first year with a clear delivered win and a public artifact
- A named next-step target for year 3

## 5. Cross-Cutting Considerations

### 5.1 Spouse Situation (do not treat as secondary)
- Every country decision must be modeled with the spouse's status. The order of operations that works best: (1) your permit approved, (2) her dependent visa filed, (3) her separate work permit or freelance pathway.
- Remote work by the spouse is the safest bridge. If she has skills that transfer to remote work (writing, design, research, teaching online), invest in that runway now.
- Kenya, Rwanda, and South Africa are the friendliest of your realistic targets for a working spouse. Ethiopia and Nigeria are the hardest.

### 5.2 Fulbright & J-1 Residency — Practical Notes
- USEFP has confirmed to you that the two-year residency can be fulfilled non-consecutively. Get this in writing on USEFP letterhead and file it.
- Time working in a third country generally does *not* count toward the residency requirement (only time in Pakistan does). Confirm this explicitly with USEFP before you commit to a multi-year Africa role.
- If you ever contemplate a J-1 waiver route, understand that a waiver forecloses future J-1 eligibility and can complicate later U.S. immigration. You've said you don't want a U.S. path — this simplifies things.

### 5.3 Salary Reality Check
- Your PKR 400,000–500,000 net target maps to roughly USD 20,000–27,000 net per year at July 2026 exchange rates. This is achievable and often beatable at local-hire scales in Nairobi, Kigali, Kampala, Accra, and Cape Town for a Program Officer or Research Associate role with a Master's.
- Fully remote INGO roles (Room to Read HQ remote, Rising Academies remote, small-org MEL) can beat this comfortably in USD terms but the competition is global.
- If you're offered materially below this in a target city, the *career trajectory* may still justify it — but not by more than one such role.

### 5.4 Risk Register
| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| U.S. aid funding cuts hollow out USAID-contractor jobs | High | Weight applications toward non-U.S.-funded orgs (UN, GPE, foundations, private ed, national-hire INGOs) |
| Work-permit denial or delay | Medium | Multiple parallel offers; keep Pakistan-based fallback role live |
| Spouse-visa complications | Medium | Filter countries by spouse-work-rights up front |
| No offer by graduation | Medium | Have 2 consulting relationships that can start remotely from Pakistan in June 2027 |
| J-1 grace period expiring | Low if planned | Confirm exit date with DSO in Feb 2027 |
| Family or health emergency in Pakistan | Low but disruptive | 3–6 month financial reserve |

### 5.5 Metrics to Track Monthly

- Applications submitted (target: 5–10/week in Phase 4)
- Interviews scheduled (target: 3–5/month by Feb 2027)
